A New Test for Acidity & Alkalinity

Little Papers That Blush and Blanch at a Drop

Professor Amelia Kersey, of the Chemical Section, has laid before the Fellows a dainty but most useful contrivance for the rapid testing of acidity and alkalinity in solutions and mixtures, whether domestic or diabolical.

By steeping fine paper in extracts of certain vegetable dyes—among them the juice of red cabbage and the liquor of the litmus lichen—she has produced slips which, upon mere contact with a liquid, change their complexion according to its temper:

Turning a modest crimson in acid company, and paling to blue when entertained by alkalies.

In a demonstration that delighted the assembled savants, a series of household fluids—lemon-juice, soap-suds, vinegar, bicarbonate draughts, and even a suspect barrel of porter—were presented to the papers, which blushed or blanched with gratifying promptitude.

“We may now ascertain, in the very scullery itself,” Professor Kersey declared, “whether a mixture tends to bite like a lawyer or soothe like a curate.”

Apothecaries foresee a brisk trade; and certain matrons have already petitioned the Professor to adapt the method to detect the moral acidity of visiting relatives. This, she regrets, must await future research.
