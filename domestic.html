Experiments with the Domestic Aethernet

Parlour in Bloomsbury Receives Moving Picture of a Cat

Bloomsbury, London

While the grand demonstrations of the Aethernet have occupied the attention of learned societies, certain private householders have been quietly adapting the system for less solemn purposes.

In a modest house off Russell Square, Miss Euphemia Bracknell has fitted to her sitting-room wall a small rectangular Aethernet screen, politely described as a “domestic cathode window.” Attached by wire to her brother’s workshop in the basement, it allows the transmission of simple images traced upon a special slate.

On Tuesday last, Mr. Bracknell, seeking to test the apparatus, drew the likeness of a cat engaged in the theft of a Christmas pudding. Within a fraction of a minute the same feline delinquent appeared, in pale green sparks, upon his sister’s parlour wall.

“It is most improving,” Miss Bracknell assured our reporter, “for I can now receive admonitory illustrations from my brother without the bother of his person.”

The Society notes with interest that, if such domestic Aethernet windows were connected to the wider network proposed by Mr. Wexley, a lady in Bath might one day receive, without delay, both the latest Paris fashions and the disapproving expression of her Aunt in Edinburgh.
